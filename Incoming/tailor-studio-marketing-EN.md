# RUSSIA THIS SUMMER

Arab travellers and Russia share something most people don't expect: a comfort with the long evening, the unhurried table, the night that starts late and doesn't apologize for it. Russia just does it in a different language — and in summer, under a sky that refuses to go dark.

Russia in summer is a different country entirely. The light changes everything. In Moscow, long evenings stretch past 10pm with the kind of golden hour that makes the city look painted. In St. Petersburg, night simply doesn't come. From late June into July, the sky stays pale and luminous past midnight — a phenomenon that exists almost nowhere else on earth at this latitude, and that no photograph has ever fully captured.

This is not a tour of monuments and museums. It's a week inside a city that works, breathes, eats well, and stays up late. Private cars, private guides, restaurants that know you're coming. No queues, no groups, no itinerary that starts at 8am.

The hotels are five-star at prices Paris would call four-star. The dining is world-class without the waiting lists. And St. Petersburg is a city that has no equivalent anywhere in the world.

# MOSCOW

## The city

Everyone arrives expecting something heavy. The architecture, the history, the general reputation. What they find instead is a city that is very much enjoying itself.

Moscow has 12 million people, a dining scene that outperforms most European capitals, and a nightlife that takes the concept of "late" more seriously than almost anywhere in the Arab world. The centre is dense, walkable, and full of people who are clearly not going home any time soon. The Kremlin is at one end of the picture; Moscow City — where a 19th century empire and a glass tower share the same skyline — is at the other. Both are real. Both are Moscow.

For Arab visitors specifically: the city is, frankly, comfortable. It understands how evenings should work. It has excellent halal restaurants that aren't an afterthought. The hookah culture runs deep. Nobody is going to look at you strangely for ordering tea at midnight and staying another hour.

## What you will do in Moscow

### The Kremlin
The Kremlin is still the Kremlin — meaning it is still the seat of government, still partially closed to the public, and still one of the most loaded pieces of real estate on earth. With a private guide and priority access, it's a different experience from what most tourists get: you move at your own pace, you go where the groups don't, and the Armoury — imperial carriages, the regalia of the Romanovs, Fabergé-era objects that would stop any serious collector in their tracks — gets the time it deserves. Worth doing once, properly.

### Sanduny
Open since 1808, never closed, never really changed. The rooms are private — booked exclusively for your group, no shared spaces, no mixed bathing. Inside: marble, dark wood, a birch whisk that an attendant knows how to use correctly, and a samovar that appears without being requested. This is not a spa treatment. Russians don't go to Sanduny to relax — they go to *reset*, which is a different thing entirely and takes about three hours. By the end, you will understand what they mean.

### Café Pushkin — first evening
The building is 19th century. The menu is Russian culinary history, written out as a dinner. The room at night — candlelit, full of people in proper clothes, unhurried in a way that Moscow restaurants don't always manage — is the right introduction to the city. Works equally well for halal and non-halal guests. Go on arrival, before the city gets familiar.

### Barashka
Azerbaijani, fully halal, and — this is not a marketing claim, it's a direct quote from a regular Arab guest — better kebabs than anything outside the Caucasus. The lamb is right. The bread arrives hot. The room is full of people who came to eat, not to photograph the interior.

### Russki — 85th floor
The 85th floor of a glass tower in Moscow City. The entire city spread below you, Russian cuisine reinterpreted through a contemporary kitchen. Moscow does this constantly — turns a corner and becomes a different version of itself. The food is worth the evening on its own terms. The view makes the point better than we can.

### Hookah — the Moscow way
Moscow takes hookah seriously — more seriously, in some corners, than anywhere in the Gulf.

There are two versions of this experience, and we offer both.

The first is straightforward: a well-run private lounge, good tobacco, a room that is yours for the evening.

The second has no name on the door. No sign, no lobby, no golden entrance. You walk up to what looks like an ordinary door, enter a code, and descend into a basement that operates entirely by its own logic. The people here are regulars. The tobacco is unlike anything available in a standard establishment — blended to order, adjusted by mood. You can say *"something red-orange, with a touch of silver"* and the person behind the counter will understand exactly what you mean. There is no equivalent to this experience anywhere outside Moscow. It is not listed anywhere. You get there because someone knows someone — and we do.

### For Muslim travellers
The Moscow Cathedral Mosque is 10 minutes from the city centre — one of the largest in Europe, capacity for 10,000 worshippers. Your guide plans the day around prayer times, not the other way around.

### Shopping — Russian designers
The interesting shopping in Moscow is not in the malls. It's in the ateliers and showrooms most visitors walk past without knowing what they contain.

**Akhmadullina** and **Akhmadullina Dreams** build collections around Russian fairy tale aesthetics — prints and silhouettes that exist nowhere else. **12 Storeez** has become one of the most followed Russian fashion brands internationally, with precisely crafted pieces that don't announce themselves. **Monochrome** for clean, considered design. A personal stylist who knows these rooms is the difference between buying something interesting and understanding what you're buying — and why it travels well.

For jewellery: **Avgvst** and **Tessa** — Russian fine jewellery with designs that are their own, at prices Western Europe can't match.

For amber: Russian amber from Kaliningrad is the finest in the world. There is no better place to buy it.

# SAINT PETERSBURG

## The city

St. Petersburg was built to impress, and three centuries later it still does. Peter the Great wanted Europe on Russian soil — so he drained a swamp, brought in architects from across the continent, and assembled a city from scratch in just over twenty years. The result is what a city looks like when someone planned the whole thing before a single stone was laid: canals, baroque palaces, granite embankments, the Neva river broad and unhurried at the centre of it all.

In June, the city is operating at full drama — and it knows it.

The White Nights are the reason to come in summer specifically. The brightest window runs June 11 – July 2, with the solstice at the centre. From late June into July, darkness simply doesn't arrive. At midnight the sky is pale gold and rose, the canals reflecting it back, and the streets that would normally be quiet at this hour are full of people sitting on steps, moving slowly, not quite ready to be inside. The city has discarded the normal rules about when day is supposed to end, and the effect is genuinely disorienting — in a way that photographs don't capture and that visitors spend years trying to explain.

There is nothing like this in the Middle East. Nothing like it within easy reach from the Gulf and Levant.

---

## What you will do in St. Petersburg

### The bridge at 1:20am
Every night from April to November, St. Petersburg's drawbridges open to let ships through the Neva. The Dvortsovy Bridge — directly in front of the Winter Palace — opens at 1:20am. Watching it from a private boat, passing underneath as the span rises, with the pale white sky above and the palace lit behind you, is the single experience in this programme that guests talk about most. There is no equivalent. Book the boat.

### The Hermitage — at the right hour
The Hermitage contains one of the greatest art collections in the world, housed in the former Winter Palace of the Romanovs. The way to see it is not first thing in the morning with four other tour groups. The way is at 5pm, when the light is good and the crowds have thinned, with a private curator who opens rooms that are not on the standard route. On certain evenings, the Peacock Clock — an 18th-century mechanical masterpiece in gold and enamel — is set in motion. It is worth timing the visit around it.

### The Fabergé Museum — private viewing
The Fabergé Museum holds the largest collection of Fabergé eggs in the world, including nine Imperial Easter eggs commissioned by the Romanov family. It occupies the Shuvalov Palace on the Fontanka river, and unlike state museums, can arrange private viewings. For guests who appreciate fine craftsmanship — gold, enamel, diamonds, mechanisms of extraordinary delicacy — this is an hour well spent.

### The canals — by private boat
St. Petersburg's canal system is the other city, visible only from the water. A private boat with a photographer, moving through Fontanka, Moika, and the Griboyedov canal in the long summer light, past 18th-century facades that have barely changed in three hundred years — this is not sightseeing. The summer light in St. Petersburg lasts for hours in the golden register. The photographs take care of themselves.

### The Gulf of Finland — at sunset (that lasts all night)
A private boat out onto the Neva and into the Finnish Gulf, with the city's skyline receding behind you — the Peter and Paul Fortress, the Winter Palace, the dome of St. Isaac's. In White Nights, the water reflects a sky that doesn't darken. Onboard catering, live music if you want it. Three or four hours that feel different from anything else in the programme.

### Nekrasova Street — the evening
One street, lined with small independent venues, each one slightly different from the last. Nekrasova has become St. Petersburg's unofficial evening quarter — not a tourist strip, but the place where the city's creative class actually spends its nights. Tiny rooms, music that comes through open doors and sometimes spills out onto the pavement, cocktails if you want them. A guide who knows which door to walk through, in which order, across two or three hours — this is the St. Petersburg that doesn't appear in guidebooks and that visitors remember.

### For Muslim travellers
St. Petersburg's Cathedral Mosque on Kronverksky Prospekt is five minutes from Gorkovskaya metro — one of the most architecturally remarkable mosques in Europe, built in 1913 with a turquoise mosaic dome modeled on the Gur-Emir mausoleum in Samarkand. Worth visiting on its own terms, not only for prayer.

### Shopping — designers and amber
In St. Petersburg: DLT, the city's flagship luxury department store, for a private shopping session with a stylist across Russian and international designers. On Nevsky Prospekt, the best selection of Russian amber — rings, necklaces, raw pieces — at prices unavailable outside Russia. For families: a session at the Imperial Porcelain Factory, where a craftsman paints a piece with your initials while you watch. It leaves with you.
